# Theorem Zero

## Part One: The First Structure

### Chapter One: The Seven-Point Residue

Choose one point and call it O. Fix the compass opening. The opening is the first preserved relation: not yet a number in the ordinary sense, but a distance held constant while the hand is still allowed to move. Place the compass point at O and sweep the first circle. Every point on the circle is one preserved opening from O. If a unit is later needed, this opening may be called one, but the unit is not imported; it is taken from the act already performed.

Choose one point on the circle and call it A. Since A lies on the circle centred at O, OA is one preserved opening. Place the compass point at A without changing the opening. Let the pencil point meet the circumference of the first circle, mark that place, and call it B. No second visible circle is needed in the main construction. The first circle supplies the boundary, the compass opening supplies the step, and B is reached because the preserved opening can be carried from A back to the circumference.

B now has two relations. It lies on the first circle, so OB is one preserved opening. It was reached from A by the unchanged opening, so AB is one preserved opening. A already lies one preserved opening from O. Therefore OA, OB, and AB are equal, and the triangle OAB is equilateral. It is not equilateral because it looks regular; it is equilateral because the construction has forced three equal sides. The radius has become a chord, the chord has become a triangle side, and the length has changed role without changing size.

Place the compass point at B, keep the opening fixed, and step again to the circumference. Mark the next point C. Continue in the same way: C gives D, D gives E, E gives F, and F returns to A. The same opening has stepped around its own circle. The circle was swept once, then its boundary was stepped. The first circle supplied the boundary, the preserved opening supplied the step, and the six steps supplied the six stations.

Let the visible circle disappear. It has supplied the boundary, allowed the unchanged opening to step, and given six stations. What remains is seven points: O at the centre, and A, B, C, D, E, F around it. These seven points are not arbitrary. They are the residue of the swept circle and the stepped opening. The circle closes relation, the sixfold step selects stations, and the disappearance of the curve reveals the seven-point residue.

Join O to A, B, C, D, E, and F. These six joins are radii. Each reaches from the centre to a boundary station, and each carries the preserved opening. They are not merely lines from the centre; they are the same distance carried in six directions. Now join neighbouring boundary stations: AB, BC, CD, DE, EF, and FA. These six joins are chords. Each joins two neighbouring boundary stations, and each was produced by the same opening that made the radius. The radius has become a boundary-to-boundary relation. The length remains; the role changes.

The seven points now produce a hexagon, but the hexagon is not the whole result. The centre and each neighbouring pair of boundary stations form a triangle: OAB, OBC, OCD, ODE, OEF, and OFA. Each triangle has two radii and one neighbouring chord. Each side is one preserved opening. Six equilateral triangles have formed around O. They were not imported, assumed, or read from appearance. They were forced by the preserved opening.

Join opposite boundary stations: AD, BE, and CF. These joins pass through O. They are diameters. Each diameter is made of two radii, so each has length two. The construction now contains three straight centre-lines: A, O, D on one line; B, O, E on one line; C, O, F on one line. The first straight angles have appeared. AOD, BOE, and COF are each 180 degrees. The angle has not been added from outside; it appears when opposite stations are joined through the centre.

Inspect the joined figure again. Some lines carry the same direction. With the present labelling, the primary parallel families are AB ∥ CF ∥ DE, BC ∥ AD ∥ EF, and CD ∥ BE ∥ FA. Each family contains three lines: one through the centre and two on either side. Direction recurs without rotation. The field has not yet been drawn as an infinite object, but field pressure has begun. A straight carrier can pass through lawful points in a repeated direction. A line can be carried without being turned.

The construction now contains a centre, six radii, six unit chords, six equilateral triangles, three diameters, three straight angles, and three primary families of parallel lines. All of it has come from one preserved compass opening. Nothing else has been added.

Now count the joins. There are seven points: O, A, B, C, D, E, and F. Any two points may be joined, so the number of possible joins is seven choose two: twenty-one. But the twenty-one joins are not twenty-one unrelated directions. They sort themselves. There are six radii, OA, OB, OC, OD, OE, and OF, each of length one. There are six neighbouring boundary chords, AB, BC, CD, DE, EF, and FA, each also of length one. Radius and neighbouring chord are different roles of the same preserved opening: one reaches from centre to boundary, the other from boundary station to boundary station.

There are six next-neighbour boundary chords: AC, BD, CE, DF, EA, and FB. These skip one station. They are longer than the unit chord and shorter than the diameter. Their exact length will later be locked as √3, but here they remain the longer chord class awaiting proof. Finally, there are three diameters: AD, BE, and CF, each passing through O and each of length two. The twenty-one joins already sort by role and length condition. Twelve joins have length one, three have length two, and six belong to the next-neighbour class whose exact value is not yet available for use.

Now count the distinct lines. The twenty-one joins do not produce twenty-one different straight carriers. Some joins lie on the same carrier. AO, OD, and AD are three joins, but they occupy one line. BO, OE, and BE occupy another. CO, OF, and CF occupy another. This collapse is not loss; it is structure. A relation may differ as a segment while sharing the same carrier. A radius and a diameter may belong to the same straight line. The segment changes; the carrier remains.

Sort the lines by direction. A direction is not the same as a line. Separate lines may still carry the same direction, as the parallel families show. AB, CF, and DE share one direction. BC, AD, and EF share another. CD, BE, and FA share another. Each family contains three separate carriers. Each preserves direction while changing position. Parallelism appears as preserved direction under offset. Translation first appears as a line carried without rotation.

The seven points are one closed local system with several readings. A join may be read by endpoints, by length, by carrier, by direction, or by role. Two joins may differ as named segments and still be equal in length. Two joins may lie on the same carrier while naming different parts of it. Two lines may be separate and still parallel. Two figures may be different instances while preserving the same relation. The question is not simply whether two things are the same. The sharper question is what kind of sameness is being preserved.

Now take triples. Any three of the seven points may be selected, giving seven choose three, or thirty-five triples. Again, the count is not the meaning. Three triples lie on diameters: OAD, OBE, and OCF. These are degenerate triples. They do not form genuine triangles; they form straight relations. AOD, BOE, and COF are straight angles. The remaining thirty-two triples form genuine triangles. A pair gives a join. A triple gives an angle-bearing figure. A degenerate triple gives a straight angle. A nondegenerate triple gives a triangle. Angle appears as soon as triples are inspected.

Separate the triples. Some use O; some do not. A triple using O is formed by choosing O and then two of the six boundary stations. There are fifteen such choices. Three are degenerate because the boundary stations are opposite: OAD, OBE, and OCF. Twelve are genuine centre-boundary triangles. Six are central equilateral triangles: OAB, OBC, OCD, ODE, OEF, and OFA. Each has side lengths one, one, one. The other six use non-neighbouring boundary stations. They belong to the catalogue, but their longer side relation remains unavailable until its length has been locked.

The triples that do not use O are boundary triangles. They are formed by choosing any three of A, B, C, D, E, and F, giving six choose three, or twenty boundary triangles. These twenty are not twenty unrelated shapes. They sort by the gaps their chosen stations make around the six-step boundary. Only three gap patterns occur: 1,1,4; 1,2,3; and 2,2,2. The pattern 1,1,4 gives three consecutive stations and produces six 30-30-120 triangles. The pattern 1,2,3 gives a triangle standing on a diameter and produces twelve 30-60-90 triangles once the right angle and side pattern have been locked. The pattern 2,2,2 gives alternating stations and produces two 60-60-60 triangles. The boundary triangle catalogue closes at six, twelve, and two.

Identity now separates from instance. A named triangle may change while the family remains. A triangle may rotate to another position and preserve its type. A local example can become a repeatable relation.

The right angle now presses forward. Whenever a boundary triangle stands on a diameter, the angle at the third boundary point is right. For example, BE is a diameter and F lies on the circle, so triangle BFE has its right angle at F. In that triangle, BE has length two and FE has length one. Once the right-angle relation is available, the next-neighbour chord BF can be locked:

BF² + FE² = BE²
BF² + 1² = 2²
BF² = 3
BF = √3

After this proof, the next-neighbour chords may be described as length √3. Before it, they may only be noticed as the longer chord class awaiting its lock. Notice may precede proof; use may not.

Ratio now appears in outline. A 30-60-90 triangle carries the side pattern 1 : √3 : 2 after the √3 length has been locked. That pattern permits stable comparison inside a right triangle. Stable comparison inside a right triangle opens the way to trigonometry, but the ratio must exist before the names can do useful work.

Coordinate pressure also begins here. The seven points have produced an origin. O is the first centre. The joins have produced direction. The parallel families have produced repeated direction. The companion carriers have produced offset. A point can now be reached by carried steps; a line can be identified by its direction family; a position can be described by movement from O. This is not yet a square grid. It is native coordinate pressure: coordinate description becomes necessary because the field has become navigable.

Algebra begins in the same disciplined way. It does not arrive first as manipulation. It arrives as memory. The drawing has produced equalities: OA equals OB, AB equals OA, and AD equals two radii. The field has produced repeated directions. The triangle catalogue has produced stable families. These relations can be written, but writing them does not make them true. The construction made them true. The symbols remember them.

One centre, one preserved opening, one swept circle, six stepped stations, the visible curve released, seven points remaining. The seven points are joined. Pairs give length classes, carriers, and direction families. Triples give straight angles, triangles, angle families, and ratio pressure. Repeated directions give coordinate pressure. Stable relations give algebraic memory. Geometry appears as visible relation. Trigonometry appears when angle and stable ratio are forced. Coordinate geometry appears when repeated direction and position require memory. Algebra appears when stable relations need symbolic compression.

The circle was swept. The boundary was stepped. The circle disappeared. Seven points remained. Those seven points generated the first local catalogue of relation. The field was not added. It began to disclose itself when the points were connected.

### Chapter Two: The Pair Catalogue

The circle has disappeared, and the seven points remain: O at the centre, A, B, C, D, E, and F around it. Nothing else is added. The first permitted act is to join two points. A pair gives a finite straight relation between named points. It may be a radius, a chord, or a diameter. It may share length with one join, carrier with another, and direction with another. A pair does not merely draw a segment; it opens a relation.

Seven points give twenty-one pairwise joins. The first six use O: OA, OB, OC, OD, OE, and OF. These are radii. Each reaches from the centre to a boundary station, and each has length one because each carries the preserved compass opening. The next six are neighbouring boundary joins: AB, BC, CD, DE, EF, and FA. These are unit chords. Each also has length one, but none is a radius. The radius reaches centre-to-boundary; the chord reaches boundary-to-boundary. Same length does not mean same role. The preserved opening remains invariant while its function changes.

The next six boundary joins skip one station: AC, BD, CE, DF, EA, and FB. These are next-neighbour chords. They are longer than the unit chord and shorter than the diameter. Their exact length will later be locked as √3, but here the catalogue may only notice the class. It may not spend the value before proof has earned it. The final three boundary joins are AD, BE, and CF. These join opposite stations, pass through O, and are made of two radii end to end. Each has length two. These are diameters.

The twenty-one joins have now sorted by role: six radii, six neighbouring unit chords, six next-neighbour chords, and three diameters. The count closes: 6 + 6 + 6 + 3 = 21. By established length, twelve joins have length one, three have length two, and six belong to the longer chord class awaiting its lock. This is already a metric vocabulary, but a disciplined one. One and two have been established. √3 has only been approached.

Now sort by carrier. A join is not the same as a line. A join is finite and has two selected endpoints. A line is the straight carrier on which the join lies. AO, OD, and AD are three joins, but they occupy one carrier. BO, OE, and BE occupy another. CO, OF, and CF occupy another. The join changes; the carrier remains. If no three of the seven points were collinear, twenty-one joins would produce twenty-one lines. But each diameter carrier collapses three joins into one line, saving two lines each time. Three such collapses save six lines. The twenty-one joins therefore occupy fifteen distinct straight carriers.

Now sort by direction. Direction is not carrier. Separate carriers may preserve the same direction. With the present labelling, the primary parallel families are AB ∥ CF ∥ DE, BC ∥ AD ∥ EF, and CD ∥ BE ∥ FA. Each family contains three straight carriers: one central member and two offset members. AB and DE are separate carriers, with CF between them as the central member. BC and EF are separate, with AD between them. CD and FA are separate, with BE between them. Position changes; direction remains. Parallelism appears as preserved direction under offset, and translation first appears as a line carried without rotation.

The seven-point residue therefore produces three primary parallel families, each with three members. These alignments are not imposed by a grid. They appear when the seven points are joined. The field has not yet been extended ring by ring, but the local figure already shows recurrent direction. Once direction recurs, the page is no longer only a drawing of one closed object. It begins to behave as a place where relations may continue. A field line, here, is only this: a straight carrier passing through lawful points in a repeated direction.

Return to the joins. OA is a radius, has length one, lies on the same carrier as OD and AD, and belongs to a central diameter line. AB is a neighbouring chord, has length one, and shares direction with CF and DE. AD is a diameter, has length two, passes through O, and shares direction with BC and EF. AC is a next-neighbour chord, belongs to the longer chord class, and waits for the √3 lock. A join is never only one thing. It has endpoints, length, role, carrier, direction, and possible family membership.

Mathematical identity depends on which relation is being inspected. Two joins may be the same in length and different in role: OA and AB both have length one, but one is centre-to-boundary and the other boundary-to-boundary. Two joins may differ as segments and share a carrier: AO and AD are different joins on the same line. Two lines may differ as carriers and share direction: AB and DE are separate but parallel. Two joins may share a family without sharing endpoints: BC and EF preserve direction without touching. The question is not merely whether two things are the same. The sharper question is what kind of sameness survives.

Same length, same carrier, same direction, same role, and same family are different claims. Ordinary sight may say that two things look alike. The construction asks what relation has remained invariant.

The pair catalogue can now be stated cleanly. Seven points produce twenty-one pairwise joins. These sort into six radii, six neighbouring unit chords, six next-neighbour chords, and three diameters. The radii and neighbouring chords have length one. The diameters have length two. The next-neighbour chords form a longer class whose exact length will be locked later. The twenty-one joins occupy fifteen distinct straight carriers. Those carriers gather into three primary parallel families: AB ∥ CF ∥ DE, BC ∥ AD ∥ EF, and CD ∥ BE ∥ FA. From pairs alone, the construction has earned length class, role class, carrier, direction, parallel family, and the first pressure toward a field.

No formula was needed first. The count came from the points. The roles came from the construction. The lengths came from the preserved opening and the diameter. The carriers came from joining. The parallel families came from repeated direction. Algebra is not absent; it is waiting as memory for relations already forced.

A pair gives a join. A join can carry length. A join can belong to a line. A line can share direction with another line. A direction can recur. A recurrent direction begins to disclose the field.

### Chapter Three: The Triple Catalogue

The pair catalogue is locked. Seven points produced twenty-one joins, and those joins sorted by role, length, carrier, and direction. The construction has earned radii, unit chords, diameters, the next-neighbour chord class, straight carriers, and three primary parallel families. The next act is to select three points. A pair gives a join; a triple gives a figure that may carry angle.

There are seven points: O, A, B, C, D, E, and F. Seven choose three gives thirty-five triples. But thirty-five triples do not give thirty-five triangles. Three triples lie on diameter carriers: OAD, OBE, and OCF. In each case the three points are collinear. A, O, and D lie on one carrier; B, O, and E on another; C, O, and F on another. These triples do not enclose area. They collapse into straight relations and give the first straight angles: AOD, BOE, and COF. Each is 180 degrees. A triple may therefore fail to become a triangle. When it does, it is degenerate.

The remaining thirty-two triples are genuine triangles. The first triple lock is therefore exact: thirty-five triples, three degenerate diameter triples, thirty-two genuine triangles.

Now separate the genuine triangles. Some use O; some do not. A triple using O is made by choosing O and then two of the six boundary stations. Six choose two gives fifteen centre-boundary triples. Three have already collapsed as diameter triples: OAD, OBE, and OCF. Twelve remain as genuine centre-boundary triangles. Six of these are central equilateral triangles: OAB, OBC, OCD, ODE, OEF, and OFA. Each has two radii and one neighbouring unit chord. Each side is one preserved opening. The other six use non-neighbouring boundary stations. They belong to the catalogue, but their longer boundary side remains unnamed by value until its length has been locked.

The triples that do not use O are boundary triangles. They are formed by choosing any three of A, B, C, D, E, and F. Six choose three gives twenty boundary triangles. These twenty are not twenty unrelated shapes. Their type is governed by the gaps between chosen stations around the six-step boundary. A chosen boundary triple divides the circumference into three positive step-gaps, and the gaps must add to six. Up to rotation and reversal, only three patterns occur: 1,1,4; 1,2,3; and 2,2,2.

The pattern 1,1,4 gives three consecutive stations, such as A, B, C. The selected points take one step from A to B, one step from B to C, and four steps from C back to A. There are six such triples, one beginning at each boundary station. These form the 30-30-120 family. The pattern 2,2,2 gives alternating stations. There are two such triples, ACE and BDF. These form the 60-60-60 family. The pattern 1,2,3 gives a triangle standing on a diameter. If B and E are opposite, then BE is a diameter and F may serve as the third boundary point. There are three opposite pairs, and each permits four third boundary stations, so there are twelve such triangles. These form the 30-60-90 family once the right angle and side pattern have been locked.

The boundary catalogue closes at six, twelve, and two: six 30-30-120 triangles, twelve diameter-standing 30-60-90 candidates, and two 60-60-60 triangles. The second triple lock is exact: the twenty boundary triangles fall into three families by gap pattern, 1,1,4; 1,2,3; and 2,2,2.

The full count now closes. There are thirty-five triples in all: three degenerate diameter triples, twelve genuine centre-boundary triangles, and twenty boundary triangles. Among the boundary triangles, six are consecutive-station 30-30-120 triangles, twelve stand on diameters and await the right-angle lock, and two are alternating-station equilateral triangles.

The pair catalogue produced length, line, carrier, and direction. The triple catalogue produces straight angle, triangle, angle family, and identity family. A pair shows that two points are related. A triple shows whether three points are collinear, whether they enclose area, and what kind of angle-bearing body they form. The straight triples give 180 degrees. The central equilateral triangles give 60-degree structure. The consecutive boundary triangles give 120-degree structure. The alternating boundary triangles give larger equilateral structure. The diameter-standing boundary triangles press toward the right angle.

Angle has not arrived as a list. It appears because triples force it. Three points either collapse into straightness or enclose a figure; once they enclose a figure, their separation around the boundary determines the family.

The right-angle family must be handled without haste. A diameter-standing boundary triangle is the doorway to the 30-60-90 relation, but the side pattern cannot be used before proof. The family may be located by its diameter. The right angle must still be locked. The length √3 must wait until the diameter-standing right-triangle proof has carried it.

Choose one representative: BFE. BE is a diameter. FE is a neighbouring chord. F lies on the circle. If the diameter forces a right angle at F, then BFE has a unit side, a diameter side, and a right angle. The remaining side can then be locked, and only then may √3 enter the ledger as an established length.

The seven-point residue has produced all triples. The triples have been sorted. The boundary triangles have been classified. The right-triangle family has been located. The next lock belongs to the angle at the boundary.

### Chapter Four: The Right Angle and the Root-Three Lock

The triple catalogue has located the diameter-standing boundary triangles. Inspect one of them: BFE. BE is a diameter, F lies on the same circle, and FE is a neighbouring unit chord. Two lengths are already established. BE has length two because it is made of two radii. FE has length one because it is produced by the preserved compass opening. The missing work is the angle at F.

A diameter-standing triangle has a right angle at the boundary point opposite the diameter. Here BE is the diameter and F is the boundary point, so ∠BFE is a right angle. The angle is not imported from a square grid. It is forced by the circle’s diameter relation: a point on a circle sees a diameter under a right angle.

Triangle BFE is now a right triangle. Its hypotenuse is BE, its known shorter side is FE, and its remaining side is BF. The right-triangle relation can now be used:

BF² + FE² = BE²
BF² + 1² = 2²
BF² + 1 = 4
BF² = 3
BF = √3

This locks the next-neighbour chord length. BF is one of the next-neighbour chords, and by rotation the same length belongs to the whole class: AC, BD, CE, DF, EA, and FB. Each has length √3.

The pair catalogue can now be completed by length. Twelve joins have length one: the six radii and six neighbouring chords. Six joins have length √3: the next-neighbour chords. Three joins have length two: the diameters. The full local length inventory is therefore 1, √3, and 2. These are not detached numbers. One is the preserved compass opening. Two is the diameter made of two radii. √3 is the side forced by the diameter-standing right triangle.

Return to BFE. Its side lengths are FE = 1, BF = √3, and BE = 2, giving the pattern 1 : √3 : 2. The shortest side is one. The middle side is √3. The longest side is two, and it is the hypotenuse. The shortest side is half the hypotenuse. The middle side is √3 times the shortest side, and √3/2 of the hypotenuse.

These are ratios, but not yet trigonometric names. A ratio is not another length. It is a comparison between lengths. Triangle BFE carries three lengths and the stable comparisons among them: one to two, √3 to two, and √3 to one. The construction has produced a ratio-bearing triangle.

The order is carried in the figure itself. The circle produced a diameter. The diameter produced a right angle at the boundary. The right triangle, with side one and hypotenuse two, forced the remaining side √3. The side pattern 1 : √3 : 2 made stable ratios visible. Only after the comparison exists can sine, cosine, and tangent have something fixed to name.

The boundary triangle catalogue can now be read with length as well as angle. The 1,2,3 gap pattern gives the diameter-standing triangles. Each has a diameter as one side and a boundary station as the third point. Each is right-angled at that third boundary point. There are twelve such triangles, each carrying the 1 : √3 : 2 pattern. The 2,2,2 gap pattern gives the alternating triangles ACE and BDF. Each side is a next-neighbour chord, so each has side length √3 and the pattern √3, √3, √3. The 1,1,4 gap pattern gives six consecutive-station triangles. Each has two neighbouring unit chords and one next-neighbour chord, giving the pattern 1, 1, √3.

The twenty boundary triangles now have a complete local ledger. Six have side pattern 1, 1, √3. Twelve have side pattern 1, √3, 2. Two have side pattern √3, √3, √3. The seven-point residue has produced a closed local catalogue of lengths and triangle families without extending the field, imposing a grid, or algebraizing the construction beyond the right-triangle relation.

The circle first supplied a boundary. The stepped boundary supplied six stations. The stations supplied joins. The joins supplied length classes and carriers. The triples supplied triangle families. The diameter-standing triangle supplied the right angle and √3. The right angle supplied ratio pressure.

The next stable question is what remains unchanged when the 1 : √3 : 2 triangle changes size.

### Chapter Five: Ratio and Similarity

The right angle has been locked, and the next-neighbour chord has been locked as √3. Triangle BFE now has side pattern 1 : √3 : 2. FE has length one. BF has length √3. BE has length two, and it is the hypotenuse because it stands opposite the right angle at F. This pattern is not decoration. It is the first stable comparison of lengths inside a right triangle.

Compare the sides. The shortest side is half the hypotenuse: 1/2. The middle side is √3/2 of the hypotenuse. The middle side is √3 times the shortest side. These are ratios. A ratio is not another segment and does not add an object to the drawing. It records how one established length stands to another established length. The 30-60-90 triangle is therefore not only a triangle of three lengths. It is a ratio-bearing structure.

Let the triangle grow. A larger 30-60-90 triangle may have side lengths 2 : 2√3 : 4, or 3 : 3√3 : 6, or 10 : 10√3 : 20. The absolute lengths change; the comparisons do not. In every case, the shortest side is half the hypotenuse, the middle side is √3/2 of the hypotenuse, and the middle side is √3 times the shortest side. This is similarity: shape preserved while size is allowed to change. The angle pattern remains. The side ratios remain. Only absolute length varies.

Scaling all corresponding lengths of a triangle by the same factor preserves the ratios between corresponding sides. If two lengths are both multiplied by the same number, their comparison does not change. Doubling both lengths changes the measures, not the fraction between them. Scaling changes magnitude while preserving proportion.

This sharpens sameness again. Congruent figures preserve size and shape. Similar figures preserve shape and ratio, but not necessarily size. Same length, same carrier, same direction, same angle family, and same ratio are different claims. The construction now distinguishes equality of measure from equality of form.

Ratio now opens trigonometry. In a right triangle, choose one acute angle. One side lies opposite that angle. One side lies beside it. The longest side lies opposite the right angle and is the hypotenuse. Three comparisons become stable: opposite to hypotenuse, adjacent to hypotenuse, and opposite to adjacent. Sine names opposite over hypotenuse. Cosine names adjacent over hypotenuse. Tangent names opposite over adjacent. The names come after the ratios. The ratios come after the right triangle. The right triangle comes after the diameter lock.

Apply the names to the 30-degree angle in BFE. The opposite side is one, the adjacent side is √3, and the hypotenuse is two. Therefore sin 30° = 1/2, cos 30° = √3/2, and tan 30° = 1/√3. For the 60-degree angle, opposite and adjacent exchange roles. Therefore sin 60° = √3/2, cos 60° = 1/2, and tan 60° = √3. These values are not memorized first. They are consequences of the locked triangle.

Trigonometry enters as the reading of a ratio-bearing right triangle. The construction gives the right triangle. The right triangle gives the side pattern. The side pattern gives stable ratios. The ratios receive names.

The circle can now be reread. At first, it was the set of points one preserved opening from O. After the right angle and ratio have been earned, a unit radius can also be read by projection. A radius may lean while its length remains one. If a right angle is used to read its across-part and up-part, then the across-part is measured by cosine of the angle, and the up-part is measured by sine of the angle. A point on the unit circle may later be recorded as (cos θ, sin θ), but that reading depends on projection, and projection depends on the right triangle. It does not replace the native triangular field. It is a perpendicular-coordinate reading of a unit radius after ratio has been earned.

The triangular construction opens trigonometry in order: diameter, right angle, side pattern, ratio, similarity, named ratios, projection. The next pressure is position. The construction has repeated directions, parallel families, unit steps, and a centre. Coordinates begin there, not in an imported square grid.

### Chapter Six: Native Coordinates

The construction now has a centre, unit steps, repeated directions, and parallel families. This is enough to create coordinate pressure. A coordinate is needed when position must be remembered. The first few points can be seen directly; a larger field cannot be held by sight alone. If the construction can continue, the route through it must be recordable.

The first coordinate language belongs to the construction itself. The triangular field does not begin with two perpendicular axes. It begins with unit directions separated by 60 degrees. Choose two neighbouring unit directions and call them u and v. Each has length one, and the angle between them is 60 degrees. A field point may be reached by taking a steps in direction u and b steps in direction v. Record that point as (a,b). The coordinate pair is not only a label. It is movement history: the memory of how the point was reached by lawful steps.

Once reverse movement is allowed, the step counts may be negative as well as positive, so a and b are integers. The coordinate system is not confined to one wedge of the field. It can move forward and backward along the chosen native directions.

Now distinguish two measures. A point may be reached by field steps, and it may also have a straight-line distance from O. These are not always the same. A first-ring point such as A is one field step from O and one straight unit from O. There the measures agree. But a point reached by one step in direction u and one step in direction v has movement history (1,1). It requires two unit field steps, while its straight distance from O is √3. The coordinate pair records route. The distance formula records straight separation. They are different measures, not competing answers.

Derive the native distance rule. Let u be the unit direction across the page, so u = (1,0). Let v be the neighbouring unit direction at 60 degrees, so v = (1/2, √3/2). A point with native coordinate (a,b) is reached by a u + b v. In perpendicular reading, its across-part is a + b/2, and its up-part is b√3/2. Its squared straight distance from O is therefore:

(a + b/2)² + (b√3/2)²

Expanding gives:

a² + ab + b²/4 + 3b²/4
The b² parts combine, so the squared distance is:
a² + ab + b²

The Euclidean distance is therefore:

√(a² + ab + b²)

The expression a² + ab + b² is squared distance, not distance. The middle term ab is not an error. It records the fact that u and v meet at 60 degrees rather than 90 degrees. The two directions lean into one another, and the algebra remembers the geometry.

Test the rule. For (1,0), the squared distance is 1, so the distance is 1. For (0,1), the squared distance is also 1. For (1,1), the squared distance is 3, so the distance is √3. For (2,0), the squared distance is 4, so the distance is 2. The first distance classes return: 1, √3, and 2. They are no longer only visible in the seven-point residue. They are searchable in the wider field. The formula did not create these distances. The construction created them. The formula carries them beyond the first drawing.

The 30-degree ray is lawful in the field, but it is not a nearest-neighbour edge direction. It appears as a carried direction through field points.

The coordinate signature is basis-relative and sector-relative. If the native directions change, the coordinate description changes. The geometric ray remains; the coordinate memory depends on the chosen directions. Native coordinates are not the field itself. They are a disciplined way of remembering movement through the field.

### Chapter Eight: Cells and Area

The field now has recurrence. Points extend in rings, directions carry through lawful stations, native coordinates record movement, and the distance rule records straight separation. But the field does not contain only points and lines. It contains cells.

The smallest repeated cell is the equilateral triangle of side one. It was already present around O, where the six central triangles formed six copies of it. The unit equilateral triangle is the first native area unit of the construction: three unit edges enclosing a surface that repeats without gaps or overlaps.

Find the area of one cell. Take an equilateral triangle of side one and drop its height to the midpoint of the opposite side. The height divides the cell into two congruent right triangles. Each half has hypotenuse one and base one half. If h is the height, then:

h² + (1/2)² = 1²
h² + 1/4 = 1
h² = 3/4
h = √3/2

The area of the cell is then:

area = 1/2 × base × height
area = 1/2 × 1 × √3/2
area = √3/4

The formula did not arrive first. The cell arrived first. The height was earned from the right-triangle relation, and the area formula records the surface enclosed by the cell.

Area can now be counted. Two equilateral cells sharing an edge form a rhombus, with area 2 × √3/4 = √3/2. Six equilateral cells around O form the central hexagon, with area 6 × √3/4 = 3√3/2. These are cell counts, not separate mysteries. One cell has area √3/4. Two cells have area √3/2. Six cells have area 3√3/2.

The construction has moved from length to surface. A length measures an edge. An area measures enclosed surface. The field makes area visible because repeated cells tile the surface. Any figure built from whole equilateral cells may be measured by counting its cells and multiplying by √3/4, provided the cells do not overlap and none are missing.

This opens decomposition. A body may be read by outline or by cells. The outline shows boundary. The cells show how surface is assembled. If a figure is made of six cells, its area is six cell-units. If the same six cells are rearranged without stretching, losing, duplicating, or overlapping them, the area remains the same. Changing outline does not necessarily change area. The invariant is surface, provided the pieces are preserved.

Area introduces another kind of sameness. Rotation, reflection, and translation preserve length and angle. Scaling preserves ratios while changing length and area. Recomposition may change outline, side relations, and orientation, but it preserves area when the same pieces remain fully accounted for. Same area is not same shape. Two figures may have different outlines and equal area. Two figures may look similar and have different areas. The invariant must be named.

The triangular field can now support area proof. A figure may be cut into cells. A triangle may be paired with another triangle to form a rhombus. A rhombus may be split back into two triangles. The preserved opening gave unit length. Unit lengths enclosed the equilateral cell. The cell gave native area. Repeated cells gave area counting. Area counting opened decomposition and recomposition. The next question is transformation: what can change, and what must remain?

### Chapter Nine: Transformations and Invariants

Recompose a figure. Recomposition differs from rotation, reflection, translation, and scaling because it does not act on a whole figure as one body. It cuts a body into pieces and rearranges them. Its invariant is area, provided the pieces are not stretched, lost, duplicated, or overlapped. A figure may change outline and still preserve surface. The body before and after may not have the same shape or boundary, but if the same pieces remain fully accounted for, the area remains.

The transformation ledger is now precise. Rotation permits turning and preserves distance, angle, and shape. Reflection permits reversal and preserves distance, angle, and shape while changing handedness. Translation permits relocation and preserves local distance, angle, direction, and shape. Scaling permits size-change and preserves angle and ratio. Recomposition permits rearrangement and preserves area under strict piece-accounting.

Each transformation must name what survives. Sameness is not one thing. Same length is not same area. Same area is not same shape. Same shape is not same size. Same ratio is not same position. Same identity family is not same instance. A claim of sameness remains incomplete until its invariant is named.

The construction now has a language for lawful change. ### Chapter Fourteen: Stations Become Roles

The circle has been reread as distance condition, boundary, station-source, intersection partner, sixfold selector, chord generator, diameter carrier, right-angle source, and catalogue perimeter. Selection gives the station temporary work. A station can be chosen with another station to form a pair, and a pair gives a join. A and D are fixed stations under reflection across AD. B and F are reflection partners. C and E are reflection partners. A station can be read geometrically as a point, constructionally as a station, metrically as part of a length relation, and algebraically as a coordinate. These readings must not be collapsed. The station is the point. The coordinate is a record.

Recurrence gives the station a local-centre role. Once the field grows, any lawful station can become the centre of a local sixfold neighbourhood. The first circle was centred at O, but another unit circle may be swept from A, from B, or from any lawful field station. Centrehood is no longer only a property of O. O is the first centre. The centre-function can travel. A station becomes a local centre when it receives the compass and repeats the unit relation.

A station may be radius endpoint, chord endpoint, neighbour, opposite, diameter endpoint, straight-angle endpoint, intersection point, triangle vertex, family member, rotation image, reflection partner, coordinate point, local centre, and proof condition. A point becomes mathematical when the construction gives it work. The letters A, B, C, D, E, and F are necessary, but the letters are not the mathematics. The roles are the mathematics.

At first, A is just a name. Then A is one unit from O. Then A can receive the compass. Then A can join to B. Then A can oppose D. Then A can form a triangle. Then A can move under rotation. Then A can remain fixed or exchange under reflection. Then A can recur as a local centre elsewhere in the field. The station is stable enough to carry changing roles.

The fixed opening stayed the same while changing role. Now the station stays the same while changing role. The construction is learning how identity survives use. A thing can remain itself while doing different work. The first inspection names the point. Reinspection asks what the point can do.

A, B, C, D, E, and F are not merely labels on a circle. They are role-bearing points in a lawful construction. Selection can now be read again. Pairs and triples are not just counts. They are acts that activate relation.

### Chapter Fifteen: Selection Activates Relation

The stations have been reread as role-bearing points. A station may be a radius endpoint, chord endpoint, neighbour, opposite, triangle vertex, rotation image, reflection partner, coordinate point, local centre, or proof condition. Selection can now be read again. At first, it seems simple: choose two points, choose three points, count the possibilities. But selection is not passive. It activates relation.

A point may exist without being joined. Two points may exist without their segment being inspected. Three points may exist without their triangle being read. The points are present; the relation becomes active when the selection is made. Selection turns available points into an inspected figure.

Choose two of the seven points. A pair gives a join, and the join may be a radius, neighbouring chord, next-neighbour chord, or diameter. Selecting two points does not merely identify two locations. It asks what straight relation lies between them. The pair opens length, carrier, direction, and role. O with A gives a radius. A with B gives a neighbouring chord. A with C gives a next-neighbour chord. A with D gives a diameter. The same act has different results because the selected points carry different relations.

The pair catalogue made this visible. Seven points produce twenty-one pairs, but the twenty-one pairs do not remain a flat list. They sort into six radii, six neighbouring unit chords, six next-neighbour chords, and three diameters. The sorting is not imposed from outside. Selection makes the relation available. Inspection names it. Proof locks what it carries.

Choose three of the seven points. A triple may produce a genuine triangle, or it may collapse into a straight relation. A pair always gives a join; a triple must be inspected to see whether it encloses area. OAD, OBE, and OCF are triples, but each lies on one carrier and therefore gives a straight angle rather than a genuine triangle. Triple selection first teaches restriction: not every three selected points form a triangle. A triangle needs three non-collinear points, and the selected points themselves force the distinction.

When three non-collinear points are selected, the figure opens. OAB uses the centre and neighbouring boundary stations, and it is equilateral. ABC uses three consecutive boundary stations, and it belongs to the 30-30-120 family. BFE stands on a diameter, and it belongs to the 30-60-90 family. ACE uses alternating boundary stations, and it belongs to the 60-60-60 family. Selection opens the figure; the construction tells what kind of figure has been opened.

The boundary triangle catalogue can now be reread. The six boundary stations allow twenty triples, but the twenty triples are not twenty unrelated triangles. They fall into three families by gap pattern. The selected stations divide the six-step boundary into three gaps, and the gaps must add to six. Up to rotation and reversal, only 1,1,4; 1,2,3; and 2,2,2 occur. Selection chooses; order interprets; proof locks. The 1,1,4 pattern activates the 30-30-120 family. The 1,2,3 pattern includes an opposite pair and activates the diameter-standing family, which locks the right angle and the 1 : √3 : 2 side pattern. The 2,2,2 pattern activates the larger equilateral family. Triangle families are named by selected relation, not appearance.

Instance and identity now separate. BFE is one triangle. ACD is another. They are different instances. But if each stands on a diameter with a third boundary station, each belongs to the same identity family. The selection differs; the relation pattern remains. An instance is a particular selected figure. An identity family is the relation preserved across several instances. Without instances, there is nothing visible to inspect. Without identity families, every figure remains isolated. Selection gives the instance. Reinspection reveals the family.

Transformation enters the same reading. A triangle may rotate to another position; the selected station names change, but the relation pattern may remain. A reflection may exchange stations; orientation changes, but side pattern and angle pattern may remain. Scaling may change length, but ratio may remain. A transformed figure may not be the same instance, yet it may preserve the same identity. The sharper question is not whether it is the same triangle, but what kind of sameness has survived: same selected names, same side lengths, same angle pattern, same ratio, same area, same family, or same constructional route.

Selection forces this question because it produces many local figures from one small station set. Difference appears first; the construction then shows what survives across difference. Mathematical sameness begins there.

Selection extends into the field. In the first circle, the station set is finite: A, B, C, D, E, and F. In the field, lawful stations recur. Pairs and triples can be selected elsewhere. A local pair may reproduce a unit edge. A local triple may reproduce an equilateral triangle. A diameter-like relation may appear in a larger or translated setting only if the required conditions are present. The field does not make every selection meaningful in the same way. It multiplies lawful opportunities for relation. Each selected relation still has to be inspected and locked.

A pair is not a unit edge merely because it is a pair. A triple is not equilateral merely because it looks balanced. The selected points must satisfy the conditions. Selection opens the question; the construction answers it.

The method is exact: select, inspect, classify, lock, reinspect. Pairs were selected, inspected, classified as radii, chords, next-neighbour chords, and diameters, and locked by length where proof was available. Triples were selected, inspected, classified as degenerate triples, centre-boundary triangles, and boundary triangles. Boundary triangles were classified by gap pattern. The right-angle family was locked by diameter. The length √3 was locked by the right-triangle relation. Selection began the process; it did not finish it.

Selection does not prove. It does not name by itself. It does not make resemblance reliable. It activates a relation for inspection. The proof must still be carried, the name must still be earned, and the invariant must still be identified.

The first movement used selection to build catalogues. The second movement sees what selection was doing. The circle gave stations. Selection activated joins and figures. Inspection sorted them. Proof locked them. Reinspection turned them into families.

Pairs and triples are not merely counts. They are acts that make relation available. Identity can now be read again. Once selection produces many instances, the question becomes unavoidable: what makes different instances belong to the same family?

### Chapter Sixteen: Instance and Identity

Selection has been reread. A pair activates a join. A triple activates a possible angle-bearing figure. Selection does not prove by itself; it opens a relation for inspection. The next question is sharper: when are two selected figures the same?

BFE is not ACD. They do not use the same station names, occupy the same place, or form the same local instance. Yet each may stand on a diameter, carry a third boundary point, satisfy the same right-angle condition, and belong to the same 30-60-90 family. They are different as instances and the same as family members. The construction now needs sharper language. An instance is a particular selected figure. An identity is the relation pattern that survives across instances. The instance is local. The identity is structural.

The twenty boundary triangles are twenty instances, but they do not make twenty identities. They collapse into three identity families: 30-30-120, 30-60-90, and 60-60-60. The triangle catalogue teaches that identity is not the same as name. A named triangle may change while the family remains.

Take the 30-60-90 family. BFE is one instance. BE is a diameter, F is the third boundary point, the angle at F is right, and the side pattern is 1 : √3 : 2. Another diameter-standing triangle may use different stations, occupy another position, and face another way. But if it stands on a diameter in the same relation, it belongs to the same identity family. The identity is not carried by the letters. It is carried by the relation.

Take the 60-60-60 family. ACE is one instance. BDF is another. They use different station sets and occupy different positions, but both use alternating stations. Their gaps are 2,2,2. Their sides are equal. Their angle pattern is equilateral. They are different as instances and same as family.

Take the 30-30-120 family. ABC, BCD, and CDE use different names, but each uses three consecutive boundary stations. Each has gap pattern 1,1,4. Each has two unit sides and one next-neighbour side. The chosen names change. The structural pattern remains. Same identity family means the governing relation pattern is preserved.

Rotation makes this visible. A sixth-turn sends A to B, B to C, and C to D, so triangle ABC may rotate to triangle BCD. The named instance changes. The family remains. If the question is whether ABC is the same named triangle as BCD, the answer is no. If the question is whether ABC belongs to the same identity family as BCD, the answer is yes. The difference is mathematical. The word same must be governed by the invariant.

Reflection gives another case. Reflect across a diameter. Some stations stay fixed. Others exchange. A triangle may reverse its orientation and face the other way, while its side lengths and angle sizes remain. Reflection may change handedness while preserving triangle identity. The instance changes; the family can remain.

Scaling gives a further distinction. A 30-60-90 triangle with side pattern 1 : √3 : 2 may grow to 2 : 2√3 : 4. The new triangle is not congruent to the first. Its side lengths are different. Its size is different. But its ratios and angle pattern remain, so it is similar. Identity must therefore be qualified. Congruent identity preserves size and shape. Similar identity preserves shape and ratio but not size. Area identity preserves surface but not necessarily shape. Family identity preserves relation pattern. Constructional identity preserves the route by which the relation is produced.

There is no single ordinary meaning of same that can do all this work. Each claim of sameness must ask what has survived. A figure may look like another and fail the required invariant. A figure may look different and still preserve the relation that matters. A reflected triangle may look reversed but preserve its side pattern. A scaled triangle may look larger but preserve ratio. A recomposed body may look unlike the original but preserve area. A named station may change under rotation while preserving the sixfold structure. Identity is not appearance alone. Identity is preservation under a specified reading.

Return to the seven-point residue. It contains many possible figures. Some are identical by length class, some by angle pattern, some by gap pattern, some by direction family, some by constructional role. Radius OA and chord AB have the same length, but not the same role. Lines AB and DE have the same direction, but not the same carrier. Triangles ABC and BCD have the same family, but not the same position. Triangles BFE and ACE are both boundary triangles, but not the same identity family. Every identity claim must say what is preserved: length, role, direction, carrier, angle pattern, ratio, area, family, or constructional route.

Identity also governs proof. A proof does not travel because two figures look alike. It travels when the proof conditions recur. Triangle OAB is equilateral because OA, OB, and AB are equal. If another triangle has three equal sides by the same constructional condition, the proof travels. The proof belongs to the condition, not to the drawing’s appearance. This is why identity family matters. It allows one proof to recur across many instances, but only when the condition is present.

The field deepens the same idea. The first circle gives a local catalogue. The field lets those relations recur elsewhere. A unit edge may appear far from O. An equilateral cell may appear far from the first six triangles. A 30-60-90 relation may appear in another position if its conditions are present. The field does not make the original instance spread everywhere. It makes the identity condition repeatable. The instance stays local. The identity can recur.

Instance answers which one. Identity answers what relation it preserves. The same selected station set gives an instance. The same relation pattern gives a family. The same proof condition lets a proof travel. The same invariant lets change remain lawful. Different named figures may belong to the same family. Similar-looking figures may fail to preserve the needed relation. The word same is not precise until the preserved relation is named.

The next question is no longer “Are these the same?” but “What kind of same?”

### Chapter Seventeen: What Kind of Same?

Identity has been reread. An instance is a particular selected figure. An identity is the relation pattern that survives across instances. The ordinary word same is too blunt. Two things may be the same in one way and different in another. A radius and a neighbouring chord have the same length, but not the same role. A radius runs from centre to boundary. A neighbouring chord runs from boundary station to boundary station. They are same in length and different in role. If the construction says only that they are the same, it loses the role. If it says only that they are different, it loses the invariant. The sharper question is: what kind of same?

Begin with same length. OA and AB both have length one. OA is a radius. AB is a chord. The construction preserves the fixed opening in both, so they are same-length relations. But they are not the same segment, not the same role, and not the same carrier. Same length means the measured distance is preserved. It does not mean the relation is doing the same work.

Same carrier is different. AO, OD, and AD lie on one straight carrier. AO is a radius, OD is a radius, and AD is a diameter. The named segments differ. Their lengths differ. Their roles differ. But their straight carrier is the same. Same carrier means selected joins lie on one line. It does not mean they have the same length or endpoints.

Same direction is different again. AB and DE do not lie on the same carrier. They are separate lines, but they carry the same direction. They are parallel. Same direction means a line has been carried without rotation. Position may change; direction remains. This is the sameness of parallelism, not sameness of place, segment, or endpoint.

Same angle pattern appears when different selected triangles preserve corresponding angles. BFE and ACD may sit in different positions and use different stations, but if each is a diameter-standing boundary triangle, each belongs to the 30-60-90 family. Same angle pattern means the angle identity remains while the named instance, orientation, or size may change.

Same ratio appears under scaling. A 30-60-90 triangle with side pattern 1 : √3 : 2 may scale to 2 : 2√3 : 4. The lengths change. The ratios remain. This is similarity. Same ratio means the comparisons between corresponding sides survive. It does not mean the sides themselves are equal. Scaling preserves ratio while changing length.

Same area appears under recomposition. A figure may be cut into pieces and rearranged. The outline may change, the orientation of pieces may change, and the resulting figure may not resemble the original. But if the pieces are unchanged, unstretched, unlost, unduplicated, and non-overlapping, the area remains. Same area does not mean same shape, boundary, or side length. It means the surface measure is preserved.

Same constructional route appears when the same lawful conditions produce the same relation. Two equilateral triangles may be produced by three sides equal to the preserved opening. One may be near O, another elsewhere in the field. Their positions and names differ, but the proof route is the same. A proof travels through conditions, not resemblance. Same constructional route is the sameness that lets proof travel.

Same identity family appears when the governing pattern is preserved across instances. ABC and BCD are different named triangles, but each uses three consecutive boundary stations, each has gap pattern 1,1,4, and each belongs to the 30-30-120 family. Same identity family does not require the same location or station names. It requires the same relation type.

Sameness under transformation must also be specified. Rotation changes position while preserving distance and angle. Reflection changes handedness while preserving distance and angle. Translation changes location while preserving local relation and direction. Scaling changes size while preserving ratio and angle. Recomposition changes outline while preserving area under piece-accounting. A transformation is not understood by saying that the figure stayed the same. It is understood by naming which relations survived and which changed. The invariant names the kind of sameness.

Resemblance can mislead. Two figures may look similar and fail to preserve the required relation. Two figures may look different and preserve the relation under inspection. A recomposed figure may look unlike the first figure and still preserve area. A reflected triangle may face the other way and still preserve its side pattern. A scaled triangle may be larger and still preserve its ratios. A chord and a radius may look like ordinary segments while differing in role. Mathematics begins when the preserved relation is named.

The seven-point residue can now be read through this question. OA and AB are same-length, different-role. AO and AD are same-carrier, different-length. AB and DE are same-direction, different-carrier. ABC and BCD are same-family, different-instance. BFE and another diameter-standing triangle are same-angle-pattern, different-position. A scaled 30-60-90 triangle is same-ratio, different-size. A recomposed body may be same-area, different-outline. Each statement is precise because it names the invariant.

An invariant is what remains unchanged under an allowed change. The allowed change may be movement, reflection, scaling, selection, recurrence, or recomposition. The invariant may be length, direction, carrier, angle, ratio, area, constructional route, or identity family. The question “What kind of same?” is therefore the question “Which invariant governs this comparison?”

This question prevents overstatement and under-reading. Without it, the construction might claim that two figures are the same when only their area is equal, that the first circle contains everything when it only releases a seed catalogue, or that visual resemblance is proof. It might also miss that a proof can travel, that a triangle identity can recur, that the field preserves direction across changed location, or that ratio survives scaling. Precision does not restrict imagination. It makes lawful imagination possible.

The word same must become a question. Same how? Same by length, direction, carrier, angle pattern, ratio, area, constructional route, identity family, or transformation invariant? Only then does sameness become mathematical. A change is lawful only when the preserved relation is known.

### Chapter Eighteen: Which Invariant Governs the Change?

Sameness has been sharpened. The question is no longer simply whether two things are the same, but what kind of same is being claimed. The next question follows: if something changes, which invariant governs the change? A change is not mathematical merely because something has moved. It becomes lawful when the preserved relation is known.

The construction began with this discipline. The compass point moved, the pencil point moved, and the drawing changed, but the opening remained fixed. That was the first governed change: move, but do not alter the opening. The permitted change was movement of the tool. The invariant was length. The preserved relation was the compass opening. Every later transformation repeats the same grammar. Something changes. Something remains. The invariant tells what kind of change has occurred.

Begin with rotation. A figure may turn around a centre. In the first circle, a sixth-turn around O sends A to B, B to C, C to D, D to E, E to F, and F back to A. Station names change position. A triangle may move to another location around the circle. A chord may move to another chord. A diameter may move to another diameter.
